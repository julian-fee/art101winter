Rigged
1500s
Fitted out with rigging of a specified kin (relates to sails in boats).
1800
The verb comes into use in the middle of the 1800s, and it comes from an earlier dialect noun that means "a trick or a swindle,"
1850
By the mid-1800s, use of the noun was beginning to slowly decline, while use of the verb and its new, broader "manipulate" meaning was rising. Suddenly anything could be rigged: contests, committees, votes. And, if the evidence is any clue, lots of things were. Game shows were rigged; juries were rigged;
1962
In 1962, future congressman John Lewis described blacks in Selma finding a “system rigged against them.” At the time, only 2 percent were registered to vote.
1972
By 1972, a former Vice President was using the word: The US tax system, Hubert Humphrey said, “is rigged against . . . the poor. And it is rigged in favor of unearned income.”
2021
Manipulated or controlled by deceptive or dishonest means.
