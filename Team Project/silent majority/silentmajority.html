  • 1st Century

    First mention (non-English) by Petronius, a roman writer: "abiit ad plures (he's gone to the majority)". Used that way for most of the 19th century, to refer to people who had died.

  • 1831

    Churchill C. Cambreleng complained to an audience about a U.S. federal bill, using the phrase to refer to representatives who voted together without closely reading the bill.

  • 1919

    Bruce Barton used the term about Calvin Coolidge’s candidacy, referring to a President that would be a spokesman for the “silent majority”, who apparently was not represented.

  • 1960s

    Richard Nixon popularized the term. He felt that a large percentage of Americans didn’t feel represented amidst the voices of dissent that largely defined the time period. This actually referred to blue collar white people who were not active in politics, a sort of “middle ground” between the two parties.

  • 2015

    Donald Trump, on the other hand, used the phrase to refer to a so-called majority of conservative voters. Despite losing the popular vote (twice) Donald Trump used the phrase throughout the presidency.
